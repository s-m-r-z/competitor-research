# Competitor Insights — 2026-07-03

## DeleteMe
- Still regarded as the most established data removal service but rated a "mid-level performer" by a 2024 Consumer Reports investigation; only ~65% removal rate versus top performers.
- Continues to offer manual, risk-prioritized removals with rescans every 90 days, from ~$10.75/month.
- Faces mounting pressure from California's free DROP platform (enforcement begins August 1, 2026), which could erode the value proposition for CA-based customers.

## Incogni
- **Major June 2026 product expansion**: launched a mobile app, expanded the Exposure Scanner to cover unlimited custom domains (not just a fixed broker list), added 24/7 support tiers, and bundled identity theft protection into the Protection Plan.
- Running aggressive July 2026 promotions: up to 58–60% off on annual plans across multiple affiliate channels, signaling a push to acquire subscribers ahead of the August DROP enforcement deadline.
- Holds PCMag and PCWorld Editors' Choice awards; touts an independent Deloitte audit of its removal practices as a trust differentiator.
- Covers 420+ data brokers with recurring requests; unlimited custom removals now included.

## Optery
- Won three categories in the **2026 Cybersecurity Excellence Awards** (Attack Surface Management, Anti-Phishing, Human Risk Management) and Best of Category in Social Engineering at the 2026 Globee® Awards for Cybersecurity — significant PR momentum.
- Currently running a **Summer 2026 Sale: 20% off** with promo code `SUMMER2026`.
- Covers 640+ brokers with automated removal and 1,000+ sites including custom removal requests.
- Remains highly rated by Consumer Reports for removal effectiveness (65%+ success rate at top tier).

## OneRep
- **Major May 2026 expansion**: added coverage of 550+ non-public data brokers (behind-the-scenes data circulation, not just public people-search sites) — a meaningful differentiator.
- Introduced **custom removal requests** (sites outside standard coverage) and **data breach monitoring** with alerts; both available via OneRep API and Pro plans.
- Achieved SOC 2 Type II compliance with independent audit validation.

## Privacy Bee
- Holds PCMag Editor's Choice award (2024–2025); remains a premium-tier offering at ~$16.42/month — nearly double some competitors.
- Covers 1,000+ data broker sites; differentiates with Google search result removal, house/property blur, a tracker-blocking browser extension, private search, and a Gmail inbox scan.
- Continuous weekly rescans and a risk-ranked dashboard are key retention hooks.
- Active comparison content being produced by rivals (notably Incogni's blog running a head-to-head comparison), suggesting competitors are actively targeting Privacy Bee customers.

## Kanary
- Transitioned from a web-based service to a **mobile-first app called "Kanary Copilot"**; previous web users retain access but new customers are onboarded exclusively through the app — a significant product pivot.
- 300+ broker coverage; hybrid automated/manual approach claims 95–100% removal rates by day 90 with monthly rescans.
- Flexible annual pricing ($14.99–$16.99/month); family add-on available.

## Removaly
- Confirmed inactive: multiple independent sources indicate Removaly ceased operations around early 2022. No new activity detected. Not a current competitive threat.

## Emerging Services
- **PrivacyOn** is gaining review coverage as a budget disruptor at $8.33/month, with dark web monitoring, 24/7 scanning, and family plans for up to 5 people. Being positioned as an "Editor's Choice" alternative in some roundups — worth monitoring.

## Regulatory / Market Context
- **California DROP enforcement begins August 1, 2026**: data brokers must access the state's free DELETE portal every 45 days, process deletion requests within 90 days, or face $200/request/day in fines. This government-run free alternative is the single biggest structural threat to paid removal services in CA.
- **US Senate investigation** found that data brokers including Comscore, IQVIA Digital, Telesign, and 6sense Insights blocked search engine indexing of their opt-out pages — heightening consumer distrust of DIY opt-outs and potentially driving demand for managed services.
- **Annual metrics reporting** (number of requests received, fulfilled, denied, and response times) now required in privacy policies by July 1 each year — a compliance pressure on brokers that could accelerate their cooperation with removal services.
- California Privacy Protection Agency (CalPrivacy) settled with two companies in early 2026 for failing to register as data brokers, signaling active enforcement.

## Market Summary
The data broker opt-out space is entering a pivotal inflection point as California's DROP platform moves from registration to enforcement on August 1, 2026, threatening to commoditize basic removal for California residents and forcing paid services to compete on speed, breadth (non-public brokers, global coverage), and bundled features like identity theft protection and breach monitoring. The arms race in feature bundling and broker coverage is intensifying, with Incogni's June expansion and OneRep's non-public broker push standing out as the most significant product moves of the past week.
