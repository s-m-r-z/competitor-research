# Competitor Insights — 2026-07-24

## DeleteMe
- Positioned at $129/year per person, with 750+ data broker coverage (automation handles 100–125 of the most active brokers; human privacy specialists manually cover the rest), having processed 100+ million opt-outs since launch.
- Added email, phone number, and credit card masking tools to the subscription at no extra charge — a product breadth play to justify premium pricing as the California DROP platform (live in 8 days) lowers the perceived value of basic opt-out submission.
- A Security.org evaluation (tested July 8, 2026 across 50+ brokers) reinforces the "mid-tier" removal rate findings from earlier Consumer Reports testing; the brand continues to win on name recognition and enterprise sales rather than measured removal efficacy.

## Privacy Bee
- Mobile app updated July 7, 2026 — no feature changelog published, but the update keeps the app current ahead of the DROP launch cycle.
- Blog published July 1 on "digital hygiene risks of sharing your data with a removal service" — a content strategy clearly aimed at seeding doubt about lower-priced, less transparent competitors without naming them directly.
- Pricing tiers in July 2026: Essentials ~$8/month, Pro ~$18/month, Signature ~$67/month (all billed annually). The Signature price point is unusually high and positions Privacy Bee as concierge-tier.

## Emerging Competitor: PrivacyOn
- **PrivacyOn is emerging as the 2026 challenger to watch**: named Editor's Choice in independent comparison reviews, priced at $8.33/month, and differentiating on B2B data broker removal (Apollo.io, ZoomInfo, RocketReach, Lusha, SignalHire, Seamless.AI) — a segment the established players largely ignore.
- B2B data broker coverage directly addresses spear-phishing and corporate cold-outreach risk, appealing to professionals and executives rather than the general consumer segment.
- Includes dark web monitoring and family plans (up to 5 people) at its base price point — features that cost more via DeleteMe or Optery.
- Coverage gap: 100+ sites currently (vs. Optery's 640+ or Incogni's 2,420+), so breadth remains a meaningful limitation.

## Regulatory / Market Context
- **T-minus 8 days to California DROP enforcement (August 1, 2026)**: The California Privacy Protection Agency's Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform goes live for consumers, requiring 600+ registered data brokers to process deletion requests within 90 days or face $200/day per unprocessed request.
- **California SB 361** (effective January 2026) expanded broker disclosure requirements, driving increased consumer awareness and top-of-funnel traffic to all removal services.
- **Connecticut** enacted a new data broker registration law modeled on California's Delete Act — the next state-level regulatory event after the DROP go-live.
- Security.org published rankings from testing 20+ data removal services (July 8, 2026), generating significant organic search traffic across all major players.

## Market Summary
With California's DROP platform 8 days from enforcement, the data broker opt-out market is at an inflection point: commoditization pressure on basic opt-out submission is pushing every major player to bundle additional value (Incogni with identity protection, DeleteMe with masking tools, Optery with Map Blurring and an open-source broker directory), while PrivacyOn quietly emerges as a credible challenger by targeting the underserved B2B data broker segment — a differentiation none of the incumbents have matched at scale.
