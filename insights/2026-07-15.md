## Privacy Bee
- No new announcements in the past 24–48 hours
- Coverage confirmed at 1,000+ data brokers (Pro/Signature tiers) with aggressive multi-channel follow-up: automated requests, legal pressure, and regulatory complaints for non-compliant brokers — differentiator that competitors rarely replicate
- Identity Vault feature (tracks name variations and aliases brokers use to rebuild profiles) remains a quiet but meaningful differentiator against services that only remove a primary identity
- Free Firefox browser extension active; no Chrome version confirmed — limits distribution reach
- Pricing confirmed stable: Essentials ~$8/mo, Pro ~$18/mo, Signature ~$67/mo (billed annually)

## Regulatory & Market Context
- **California DROP enforcement: T-17 days (August 1, 2026)** — data brokers must begin processing deletion requests every 45 days and complete removals within 90 days or face $200/consumer/day fines; 260,000+ consumer deletion requests already queued; $200/request/day liability is driving accelerated broker compliance that benefits all removal services
- Removaly alternatives content is now a high-volume SEO category; Incogni captures the majority of inbound traffic from former Removaly and Mozilla Monitor Plus subscribers

## Market Summary
Today's competitive landscape is quiet on breaking announcements — the primary story remains California DROP enforcement arriving in 17 days, which is a rising tide that lifts conversion rates across the category as consumer awareness of data-removal services accelerates. Optery holds editorial pole position (CNET Best Overall), Incogni leads on broker-count credibility and Deloitte verification, Kanary is carving a mobile-first/settings-automation niche, and Aura is the sleeper threat with its bundled all-in-one expansion. No competitor has yet run a CA-DROP-specific marketing campaign, leaving an open window for whichever service moves first.
